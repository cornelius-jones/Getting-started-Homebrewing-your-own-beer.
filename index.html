Getting started Homebrewing your own beer.

Beginners guide (from a beginner) to the brew day process.

If you've ever wanted to learn how to brew your own beer, look no further! This site contains the recommended steps to get you through brew day using the all-extract method.

01. Beginners Equipment

  • Boil Pot - The entire boiling process, including extract, hops and/or other ingredients, takes place in the boil pot, or kettle. 2.5 gallon is recommended.
  • Hydrometer - A hydrometer is used to measure the gravity, or sugar density in wort and beer.
  • Bung and Airlock - An airlock is inserted in the top of a fermenter and allows carbon dioxide, to escape the fermenter without letting contaminants in.
  • Carboy - Carboys are glass jugs, much like water cooler bottles, that brewers use for making beer, wine, hard cider & mead.
  • Funnel (optional)
  • Fermenter - A vessel is needed to hold the wort as it ferments into beer.
  • Heat Source - The stove in your kitchen should suffice for smaller amounts, while a turkey fryer or another powerful heat source works well for larger batches.
  • Stir Spoon - During the boil, stirring is required for a variety of reasons. Plastic, wooden, and stainless steel spoons are commonly used.
  • Thermometer - Be sure to acquire a calibrated thermometer that can withstand high temperatures.

02. The Importance of Sanitizing

  • Cleaning and sanitizing your homebrew gear is important because it is one of the easiest ways to ensure your beer’s quality between small batches. When you don’t clean and sanitize your homebrew gear, your beer might be exposed to microorganisms that can rapidly skunk your beer.
  • Some common bugs that can burst your beer bubble include Lactobacillus. This creature can leave your beer tasting sour (but not in a good way) because of the lactic acid it produces after eating the yeast and sugar in your beer. Other creepy creatures like Pediococcus leave mysterious rope-like bits growing in your beer.
  • Simply put, by not properly cleaning and sanitizing your homebrewing equipment, you’re setting yourself up for failure. If you clean but don’t sanitize, you open your beer up for bacteria to fester and infect your beer. If you sanitize but don’t clean, you might have leftover sediments and yeast in your new batch of beer that clouds its color and character.

03. Malt extract brewing

  • All-extract homebrewing is particularly beneficial to beginners and brewers with time, space, or budget constraints.
  • It omits one major step of all-grain brewing (the mash) and the brewday is shorter.
  • Malt extract is sold in both liquid (Liquid Malt Extract 'LME') and powdered forms (Dry Malt Extract 'DME').
  • DME is produced by heating the liquid extract and spraying it from an atomizer in a heated chamber. Strong air currents keep the droplets suspended until they dry and settle to the floor.

04. The Boil

  • Start with pouring 2½ gallons of clean water into the brew pot and begin heating it up.
  • Pour grains into grain bag and tie loose knot at the top of the bag.
  • Bring the water to a gentle, rolling boil.
  • Once the water has reached a temperature of 150°-165°, place the grain bag in the brew pot and let steep for around 20 minutes. It starts to release the sugars that the yeast will convert to alcohol. Notice in the video the importance of cleaning at all stages of the brewing process.

06. Adding Hops

  • After the liquid reaches a rolling boil, add the bittering hop addition.
  • All beers do have at least one hop addition for bitterness, to balance the sweetness of the malt.
  • When you have a kit that comes with 2 oz. of Cascade hops and wonder which one is for bitter, and which one is for aroma, they are the same. 1 oz. gets added at the beginning of the boil, while the other ounce gets added right at the end of the boil.
  • Most recipes will tell you when to add your hops either in time from the start of the boil, or time that is left in the boil.

08. Chilling the Wort

  • After the 30 minute boil is complete, you have successfully created wort, the unfermented liquid that will turn into beer.
  • Fill the clean and sanitized fermenter half way with cold water.
  • Add the hot wort in a sink filled with ice water after it has sat for 5 minutes.
  • Fill the fermenter with cool water to reach 5 gallons, and allow the wort to sit until it drops in temperature to 70-75°F (21-24°C).

09. Transfer to Fermenter

  • Pouring the wort through a stainless steel strainer can help with limiting the amount of trub that is passed over when moving the wort into the fermenter. If you are siphoning the cooled wort from the boil pot, then whirlpooling can help.
  • Whirlpooling is a means of gathering most of the trub into the center of the pot to make it easier to draw off wort from the side of the pot. Rapidly stir the wort in a circular manner. Continue stirring until all the liquid is moving and a whirlpool forms.
  • Stop stirring and let the whirlpool slow down and settle for 10 minutes or so. The whirlpooling action will form a pile in the center of the pot, leaving the edge relatively clear. The siphon won't clog as quickly now if it draws from the side of the pot.

10. Pitch and Seal

  • Sanitize the outside of the yeast bag before cutting it open.
  • Sprinkle the contents of the yeast bag over the top of the wort surface and stir with a sanitized spoon.
  • Secure the lid onto the fermenter and place the airlock and bung on the top of the fermenter.

11. Index of terms

  • Beer - Any beverage made by fermenting a wort made from malted barley and seasoned with hops.
  • Hops - Hop vines are grown in cool climates and brewers make use of the cone-like flowers. The dried cones are available in pellets, plugs, or whole.
  • Boil - The stove in your kitchen should suffice for smaller amounts, while a turkey fryer or another powerful heat source works well for larger batches.
  • Sanitize - To reduce microbial contaminants to insignificant levels.
  • Wort - The malt-sugar solution that is boiled prior to fermentation.
  • Malt - The principal source for fermentable sugar in beer.
  • Trub - A layer of sediment forms at the bottom of the fermentation vessel. This sediment comprises all the unfermentable products in wort such as fats, proteins, hops, and dead yeast.
  • Mash - The hot water steeping process which hydrates the barley, activates the malt enzymes, and converts the grain starches into fermentable sugars.
  • Hydrometer - A hydrometer is used to measure the gravity, or sugar density in wort and beer.
  • Bung and Airlock - An airlock is inserted in the top of a fermenter and allows carbon dioxide, to escape the fermenter without letting contaminants in.
  • Carboy - Carboys are glass jugs, much like water cooler bottles, that brewers use for making beer, wine, hard cider & mead.
  • Funnel (optional)
  • Fermenter - A vessel is needed to hold the wort as it ferments into beer.
  • Heat Source - The stove in your kitchen should suffice for smaller amounts, while a turkey fryer or another powerful heat source works well for larger batches.
  • Stir Spoon - During the boil, stirring is required for a variety of reasons. Plastic, wooden, and stainless steel spoons are commonly used.
  • Mash - The hot water steeping process which hydrates the barley, activates the malt enzymes, and converts the grain starches into fermentable sugars.
  • Pitching - Adding the yeast into the chilled wort.
  • Liquid Malt Extract (LME) - Concentrated sugar extracted from malted barley in a liquid, syrup form.
  • Dried Malt Extract (DME) - Concentrated sugar extracted from malted barley in powder form
  • IBU (International Bitterness Units) - a chemical measurement of the number of bittering compounds, specifically isomerized and oxidized alpha acids, polyphenols, and a few other select bittering chemicals, that make your beer taste bitter.
  • Fermenting - The total conversion of malt sugars to beer. A vessel is needed to hold the wort as it ferments into beer.
  • Brew Day - When all the fun happens. The day that the primary beer-making process takes place.
  • Original Gravity - The original gravity is the specific gravity measured before fermentation.
  • Final Gravity - The final gravity is the specific gravity measured at the completion of fermentation.
